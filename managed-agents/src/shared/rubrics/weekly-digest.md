# Weekly-digest rubric

Grades a collection's weekly digest — the `title`, `intro`, and markdown `body`
generated by `packages/ai/src/collection-weekly-digest.ts` for one ET calendar
week across a curated collection's member products. The production prompt
(`SYSTEM_PROMPT`) is the canonical guide for the model; this rubric is the
parallel artifact for the grader.

The artifact is the three fields plus the week's input releases (collection
name, week-start date, and the `org / product: title — summary` lines,
including each release's stated importance score, fed to the model). Score
each criterion independently against those inputs — do not infer facts the
inputs do not state.

## 1. Faithfulness

- Every factual claim in `intro` and `body` is supported by the provided
  release inputs. An invented feature, date, number, or capability not
  present in any input release is a fail.
- No contradiction: the artifact never describes a deprecation as an
  addition, or a removal as a feature.
- Every product or org named in the body is a real member of the input
  release list — no invented or misattributed products.
- When body text characterizes the omitted long tail (releases beyond the
  cited set), that characterization is a fair generalization of the kinds of
  releases actually present in the input, not fabricated specifics.

## 2. Coverage judgment

- The releases with the highest stated importance scores in the input are
  the ones given narrative weight (named specifically, discussed in a
  sentence or more) — not buried in a passing mention while a lower-importance
  release gets more space.
- The long tail of lower-importance / routine releases is characterized
  collectively ("a handful of routine CLI patches also shipped") rather than
  itemized one by one.
- A week with several high-importance releases should reflect that breadth —
  the body should not fixate on only one story when the inputs show multiple
  independently significant developments.
- Pure noise (dependency bumps, internal tooling, checksum-only releases) is
  skipped or folded into a supporting sentence, never given its own
  narrative beat.

## 3. Narrative quality

- The body reads as a cohesive mini blog post with a thematic
  through-line — not a reformatted bullet list or a release-by-release
  recap with no connective tissue.
- Section headings name real themes of the week's news (e.g. "Faster local
  development", "Security hardening across the SDKs") — not generic
  placeholders like "Overview" or "Releases".
- Sections group related releases meaningfully — releases within a section
  share a real thematic connection, not just co-occurrence in the same week.
- Bullet-formatted lines are a minority of the body; prose paragraphs carry
  the narrative. A body that is mostly a bulleted list is a fail on this
  criterion regardless of factual accuracy.
- The title names the week's theme (or leads with the dominant release) —
  not a formulaic "Week of <date> digest" label.

## 4. Voice

- Neutral third-party editorial tone — the digest describes what shipped and
  why it matters, the way a technology publication would, not the way a
  vendor announces its own product.
- No vendor-marketing superlatives ("game-changing", "revolutionary",
  "seamless", "best-in-class") leak into the prose, even when a source
  release's own summary used that language.
- No first-person vendor voice ("we're excited to announce", "our team")
  leaks from the source release summaries into the digest's voice.
- The intro stands alone as a meta-description: it names the week's most
  significant development(s) concretely, with no opening filler ("This
  week,").
